TULIPS

Tulips are perennial, bulb-forming spring flowers (genus Tulipa) in the lily family, renowned for their cup-shaped blooms in nearly every color, including deep purples and near-black. Native to Central Asia, they became a cultural symbol in the Ottoman Empire before creating a massive "tulip mania" economic bubble in the 17th-century Netherlands. They are popular garden plants requiring autumn planting and well-drained soil, with over 3,000 cultivated varieties.


Aside

Tulips are spring-blooming perennial herbaceous bulbiferous geophytes in the Tulipa genus. Their flowers are usually large, showy, and brightly coloured, generally red, orange, pink, yellow, or white. They often have a different coloured blotch at the base of the tepals, internally.


Article 2

Key Characteristics and Facts

  • Appearance: Usually, one bell-shaped flower grows per stem, with two to six thick, bluish-green leaves.
  • Varieties: There are numerous types, including Single Early, Double (peony-flowering), Parrot (ruffled), and Fringe tulips.
  • Colors: Available in red, yellow, purple, white, pink, orange, and near-black (e.g., 'Queen of the Night').
  • Cultivation: Bulbs should be planted in autumn for spring blooms, thriving in sunny, well-drained locations.
  • Symbolism: Generally symbolize perfect, deep love, but also represent renewal (Persian/Turkish tradition) and wealth.

History and Significance

  1. Origins: Originally from the harsh climates of Central Asia, they were cultivated by the Ottomans from the 10th to 16th centuries.
  2. Tulip Mania: In the 1630s, the Netherlands experienced a speculative bubble where single bulbs sold for more than a house.
  3. Modern Industry: The Netherlands remains the world's largest producer and exporter of tulips.
